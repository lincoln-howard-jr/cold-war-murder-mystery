Character Brief: First Lady
Name: Eleanor Mercer
Age: 55
Career: Former arts attorney and White House cultural advisor
Hometown: Richmond, Virginia
Education: Smith College, JD from Georgetown University
Backstory
Public life trains people to notice the wrong things. They notice gowns, handshakes, applause lines, and whether the candles were lit on time. I notice who stops smiling when a certain name is mentioned. I notice which host overexplains a seating chart. I notice when a room begins performing normalcy a little too aggressively.

That instinct has kept my husband out of more trouble than half the men who brief him each morning. It has also made me useful in quieter ways. I built cultural initiatives, restored public arts programming, and turned state dinners into instruments of diplomacy rather than expensive meals with better china. That is how I came to know Anna Whitmore. She understands presentation as well as anyone in Washington. Which is why her composure tonight troubles me.

Charles Whitmore has always mistaken elegance for absolution. He believes a string quartet, donor list, and historical anecdote can soften any hard edge. Usually he is right. Tonight he may have overreached. The guest list is crowded with exactly the sort of people who turn an anniversary into an investigation.

The men of security are tense in their own distinct dialects. Raymond sees geometry. Marcus sees vibes and somehow keeps being right about them. Victor Hale is orbiting too close to power for comfort. The young reporter has the expression of a man hoping dessert comes with a scandal. And Talia Jane is polished in the way ambitious women often are when they are concealing impatience.

My husband wants to be gracious. I want him to be safe, unsullied, and in the car before anyone else's bad choices become ours. If this evening stays ceremonial, I will help it sparkle. If it turns, I will be the first one to say we are leaving.
Objective
Protect the President socially and politically while keeping the evening graceful.

Before the midnight toast, determine which poses the greater danger to the administration: a security failure, a corruption scandal, or a personal scandal. Use charm, conversation, and observation to steer your husband away from the worst of it without causing a public scene.
